# Checklists

Use these checks before finalizing research notes, reviews, or synthesis outputs.

## 1. Self-containedness check

- Have all nontrivial symbols been defined before use?
- If a formula is central, is the full minimal formula shown rather than only described?
- Can a mathematically comfortable beginner follow the logic without leaving the document?
- After each dense formula block, is there a short intuition or toy explanation?

## 2. Integration check

- Is the paper explicitly connected to the local roadmap or map?
- Is its nearest neighbor in the existing corpus named directly?
- Is the key difference stated at mechanism level rather than topic level?
- Is the paper placed on stable axes instead of vague impressions?

## 3. Critique check

- Is the main assumption identified?
- Is the practical or statistical cost identified?
- Is the most likely failure mode identified?
- Is the claimed novelty distinguished from actual system impact?
- Is there at least one concrete research opening, not just a generic future-work sentence?

## 4. Symbol check

- Are overloaded symbols such as `A_i`, `B_i`, `lambda`, `phi`, `q`, or `r_i` locally disambiguated?
- If two papers use the same symbol for different objects, is that difference stated explicitly?
- Are global symbols separated from paper-local symbols?

## 5. Literature review check

- Is the review organized by a defensible synthesis logic instead of chronology?
- Are the section boundaries meaningful for later retrieval and research use?
- Does each section compare mechanisms, not just applications?
- Are open tensions stated at the level of representation, injection, optimization, or evaluation?

## 6. Research usefulness check

- Could the user tell which paper to revisit for a specific question?
- Could the user tell which idea is most reproducible as a toy?
- Could the user tell which direction is promising but high-risk?
- Could the user tell what to read next if they want to make an original contribution?
